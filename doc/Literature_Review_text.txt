Literature Review: Limitations of BirdNET, Google Perch, and Existing Bioacoustic Research1. BirdNET: The Dominant Off-the-Shelf SolutionBirdNET, developed collaboratively by the Cornell Lab of Ornithology and Chemnitz University of Technology, represents the most widely deployed deep learning system for automated bird vocalization identification. The system classifies 48kHz 3-second audio clips into 3,337 classes using a convolutional neural network (CNN) trained on scaled spectrograms computed via short-time Fourier transform. The class set is primarily composed of bird species but also includes non-bird categories such as environmental noise and human voices.BirdNET's architecture has evolved across versions: early releases (e.g., the original app model) were built upon a Wide-ResNet foundation, while the current BirdNET-Analyzer V2.4 employs an EfficientNetB0-like backbone (with a 1024-dimensional embedding), capable of identifying 984 North American and European bird species in its primary deployment. The model processes audio signals into two log-scaled mel-spectrograms, visualizing frequency patterns from 0–3 kHz and 150 Hz–15 kHz for detailed analysis, which are then fed into the CNN. The model is optimized for robustness against background noise while maintaining efficiency for real-time mobile use.Despite its widespread adoption, BirdNET exhibits several critical limitations documented in the literature:Performance Degradation Under Noisy Conditions. BirdNET is known to struggle with noisier recordings, reducing its accuracy for passive acoustic monitoring (PAM). Research on Boreal Owl vocalization detection demonstrated that both custom-built CNNs and BirdNET exhibited similar performance drops when vocalizations had a signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) inferior to 3 dB. Training models on low-SNR vocalizations did not guarantee detection at test time, highlighting that SNR distribution limitations make a data-centric approach crucial.Geographic and Species-Specific Bias. A global evaluation of BirdNET using 4,224 one-minute soundscapes from 67 sites across 28 administrative regions—annotated by local experts and encompassing 1,020 species—revealed significant performance variability across geographic regions, biomes, and species. While precision was relatively consistent across continents (range: 0.57–0.71), recall was significantly lower and more heterogeneous (range: 0.24–0.52). Predictive power (PR AUC) was highest in North America, Oceania, and Europe (0.16–0.23) but dropped to 0.03–0.04 in Africa and Asia. BirdNET performed poorly in Costa Rica and Brazil, where bird species remain underrepresented in global acoustic libraries. The model effectively identifies common species but demonstrates significant inefficiency with "not so common" species.Label Noise and Dataset Imbalances. Systematic investigation of BirdNET misclassification causes identified model limitations, dataset imbalances, signal noise, and incorrect labels as primary factors. Dataset analyses revealed significant class imbalances and the presence of label noise, particularly in challenging species where false positives are more prevalent. Controlled experiments demonstrated that reducing label noise in training sets can improve F1 scores.Lack of Standardized Noise Testing. The BirdNET ecosystem includes the BirdNET-Analyzer, a modular system designed for scientific audio data processing. However, the native workflow lacks a standardized, quantifiable pipeline for controlled noise testing—a gap that limits systematic evaluation of model robustness under varying acoustic conditions. The model only passively processes unquantified, random natural field noise without systematic evaluation across graded SNR conditions.2. Google Perch: A Parallel Bioacoustic Foundation ModelGoogle's Perch model represents another major off-the-shelf solution for bioacoustic species classification. The base Google Perch model employs an EfficientNet-B1 architecture—marginally larger than the EfficientNetB0-like backbone used by BirdNET V2.4—and was primarily trained on Xeno-Canto (XC) recordings.Perch 2.0, the latest iteration introduced by Google DeepMind and Google Research, expands from training exclusively on avian species to a large multi-taxa dataset. The model is trained with self-distillation using a prototype-learning classifier alongside a novel source-prediction training criterion. Perch 2.0 achieves state-of-the-art performance on two bioacoustics benchmarks: BirdSET and the BEnchmark of Animal Sounds (BEANS). It also outperforms specialized marine models on marine transfer learning tasks, despite having almost no marine training data.Notably, Perch 2.0 provides both off-the-shelf classification scores for thousands of vocalizing species and strong embeddings for transfer learning. The model generates 1536-dimensional embeddings with full support for EfficientNet-B3 bioacoustic architectures.A key finding from the Perch 2.0 development—framed as the "bittern lesson"—is that simple, supervised models remain difficult to beat. While self-supervised learning approaches such as Bird-MAE, BirdAVES, and SimCLR-style models have been explored in the literature, these approaches generally struggle to outperform strong supervised baselines. The Perch team's own exploratory research with MAEs, HuBERT, and SimCLR yielded a similar inability to consistently outperform supervised models.However, several limitations persist:Limited Generalization to Under-Studied Species. The Google Bird Vocalization (GBV) Classifier achieves only approximately 60% accuracy on the BirdCLEF+ 2025 competition test dataset, as the competition includes bird species that lie outside the GBV classifier's training set. This highlights the model's limited generalization to species not well-represented in its training corpus.Architectural Rigidity. Like BirdNET, Perch relies on fixed CNN architectures (EfficientNet) trained on large-scale datasets. The "bittern lesson" acknowledges that current foundation models, despite their scale, may not fully address the core challenges of low-resource and noisy-environment classification.Lack of Unified Benchmarking Framework. Neither Perch nor BirdNET provides a unified framework for comparing heterogeneous ML approaches (traditional tabular ML, image CNNs, raw-audio transfer learning) on identical datasets with controlled experimental conditions. Both models share a common limitation: they lack systematic, controllable evaluation pipelines for noise robustness.3. BirdCLEF Competitions: Benchmarking Bird Audio ClassificationThe BirdCLEF series, organized as part of the LifeCLEF lab, has become the premier benchmarking platform for bird vocalization classification. These competitions consistently highlight the core challenges facing the field.BirdCLEF 2021 specifically targeted automated avian vocalization identification in continuous soundscape recordings, aiming to advance the extraction of signals of interest from massive amounts of unexamined acoustic data. The challenge identified the exact challenge faced by researchers monitoring ecosystems: efficiently and reliably detecting and classifying bird calls within continuous audio windows to improve precision and recall for biodiversity tracking.BirdCLEF 2022 specifically targeted rare and endangered bird species recognition in soundscape recordings, aiming to advance automated detection of species that lack large amounts of training data. The challenge identified the exact challenge faced by scientists monitoring rare birds: identifying species in long recordings given limited training data.BirdCLEF 2023 focused on classifying African bird species in 10-minute-long soundscapes. The training dataset comprised 16,941 tracks spanning 192 hours of audio. A significant challenge was the species distribution, with many species having only one or two examples—a classic long-tail distribution problem.BirdCLEF 2024 shifted focus to under-studied Indian bird species in the Western Ghats, a biodiversity hotspot. Participants were tasked with identifying 182 species from a hidden test set of approximately 1,100 audio clips. A primary challenge was the significant domain shift between Xeno-Canto training recordings and passive acoustic monitoring (PAM) test soundscapes. This shift is exacerbated by differences in recording equipment, recording conditions, and background noise. Additionally, Xeno-Canto recordings are not expert-labelled at the segment level, lacking precise labels for each five-second interval.Team Epoch IV's solution to BirdCLEF 2024 highlighted several key approaches: comprehensive data augmentations, frequency and time masking, contrast adjustments, and pre- and post-processing techniques to enhance model robustness and generalization. Their findings emphasized that models exhibited a tendency to overfit, failing to generalize well to hidden test data.BirdCLEF+ 2025 continues these challenges, with participants tackling 10-second recordings of Indian bird species. A notable trend is the increasing use of foundation models like BEATs, which achieved the highest competition score of 0.913 (F1-score) when combined with pseudo-labeling.BirdCLEF+ 2026 specifically targeted understudied wildlife species identification in the Pantanal wetlands within continuous passive acoustic monitoring (PAM) data, aiming to advance biodiversity tracking in remote and threatened ecosystems. The challenge identified the exact challenge faced by conservation scientists: efficiently detecting and classifying species vocalizations within continuous audio windows given massive volumes of unexamined soundscapes and limited labeled training data.These competitions collectively underscore the field's persistent challenges: long-tailed class distributions, domain shift between training and deployment environments, noisy field recordings, weak labeling (labels present at recording level rather than duration level), and the difficulty of generalizing to under-studied or rare species.4. Noise Robustness in Bioacoustic ClassificationEnvironmental noise remains one of the most formidable obstacles to reliable bird vocalization classification. Audio data presents unique challenges for deep learning, including high signal-to-noise variability, temporal dependencies of events, and recording variability. Avian bioacoustics exemplifies these challenges, requiring models to manage diverse and noisy acoustic environments while dealing with class imbalance.Research has demonstrated that preprocessing audio with noise reduction models can improve classification accuracy, particularly for low birdsong-background ratios. However, in some cases, removing noise has been shown to make classification probability worse, suggesting that noise-robust features are not always enhanced by simple denoising.Data augmentation strategies have emerged as a primary technique for improving noise robustness. Augmentation schemes incorporating real environmental noise under various signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) conditions have been proposed to construct training, validation, and test sets. Common augmentation techniques include changes in sound-to-noise ratio, where the SNR is adjusted so that bird calls appear louder or quieter relative to background noise. Studies on the BirdCLEF 2021 dataset demonstrated that integrating various noise samples and non-bird audio elements significantly improved model performance.Generative AI-based data augmentation has recently emerged as a promising direction. Research investigating Auxiliary Classifier Generative Adversarial Networks (ACGAN) and Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs) as augmentation tools to synthesize spectrograms has shown potential for improving classification in noisy environments. Additionally, unsupervised sound separation models like MixIT, when trained specifically for birdsong data, have demonstrated improved separation quality—outperforming general audio separation models by over 5 dB in SI-SNR improvement.Despite these advances, a critical gap remains: no standardized, reproducible experimental paradigm exists for systematically comparing model performance across clean baseline audio and programmatically generated gradient noise conditions. Most studies either test single architectures or compare multiple CNN variants without integrating traditional machine learning approaches for horizontal benchmarking.5. Low-Resource and Long-Tail Species ClassificationThe long-tailed distribution of bird species in natural datasets—where a few common species dominate while most species have very few training examples—poses a fundamental challenge for classification systems. Studies comparing convolutional neural networks and few-shot learning approaches (Siamese Neural Networks) for classifying long-tailed distributed tropical bird songs have sought to develop methodologies that accurately identify both common and rare species.The low-resource scenario is particularly acute for rare and endangered species. An automated pipeline for few-shot bird call classification has been developed specifically for rare species absent from large publicly available classifiers like BirdNET and Perch. These approaches aim to refine one-shot or few-shot classification accuracy for extremely rare bird species, particularly in scenarios where only minimal field data are available.Transfer learning has emerged as a promising strategy for low-resource scenarios. Global birdsong embeddings extracted from audio classification models trained on bird vocalization data have consistently enabled higher quality classification than embeddings trained on general audio datasets. Pre-training on AudioSet followed by fine-tuning for birdsong has been shown to boost low-resource performance.However, existing research rarely provides systematic comparisons across fundamentally different algorithmic paradigms on identical datasets. The few studies that do compare multiple approaches—such as those comparing fine-tuned BirdNET, Random Forest, ResNet50, and MobileNetV2—typically focus on deep learning variants rather than including traditional machine learning baselines.6. YAMNet and Audio Transfer LearningYAMNet is a pre-trained deep learning model for sound classification, capable of classifying audio into 521 sound categories, including various noise types. The model uses a MobileNetV1 architecture and is trained to predict AudioSet classes. YAMNet and VGGish are both convolutional models trained on AudioSet, providing robust feature embeddings suitable for transfer learning.Transfer learning with YAMNet has been successfully applied across various domains. Studies have adapted YAMNet for enhanced speech command recognition, compact noise classification in urban and wireless systems, and even robust pool boiling regime classification. The typical transfer learning workflow involves replacing the original classification head with a custom dense layer matching the target number of classes.For bioacoustic applications, comparisons between general-purpose models (VGGish and YAMNet) and avian bioacoustic models (Perch and BirdNET) have shown that domain-specific models consistently outperform general-purpose models when dealing with complex or ambiguous calls. Nevertheless, YAMNet's pre-trained representations offer a valuable baseline for raw audio waveform classification, particularly in scenarios where computational resources are limited or where fine-tuning domain-specific models is infeasible.7. Research Gap SummaryThe literature review reveals several critical research gaps:First, no unified open-source framework simultaneously compares traditional tabular machine learning, lightweight image CNN, and raw-audio transfer learning approaches on identical bird audio datasets. Existing studies either test single deep learning architectures or compare multiple CNN variants; few integrate gradient-boosted tree models for horizontal benchmarking. The comparative review of foundation models for bioacoustics focuses primarily on deep learning models (Perch 2.0, BirdMAE, BEATs) without including traditional ML baselines.Second, mainstream off-the-shelf tools (BirdNET and Google Perch) both rely on fixed CNN architectures trained on large-scale datasets. Neither provides a standardized, quantifiable pipeline for controllable noise testing. BirdNET only passively processes unquantified, random natural field noise, lacking systematic evaluation across graded SNR conditions.Third, severe performance degradation occurs on low-resource rare bird species with insufficient training samples. While few-shot learning and transfer learning approaches have been explored, systematic comparison across algorithmic paradigms for rare species classification remains limited.Fourth, no reproducible controlled-variable experiment exists to separate clean baseline audio and programmatically generated gradient noise for systematic robustness comparison. Most noise robustness studies employ ad hoc augmentation strategies without standardized evaluation protocols across graded SNR conditions.Fifth, BirdCLEF competitions have consistently identified domain shift between focal (training) and soundscape (test) recordings as a primary challenge. Audio recordings are often weakly labeled (label present at recording level rather than duration level), complicating model training and evaluation. Environmental noise, overlapping vocalizations, and missing labels further compound these challenges.8. Contribution of This ProjectThis project directly addresses these gaps by implementing three fundamentally distinct algorithms—LightGBM (tabular gradient boosting), FastAI lightweight CNN (mel-spectrogram image classification), and fine-tuned YAMNet (raw audio transfer learning)—on one unified dataset with a standardized controlled-variable experimental paradigm. The design includes:Baseline Control Group: High-SNR (≥15 dB) clean recordings screened from within the representative 2,000-clip stratified subset to measure native classification accuracy under ideal conditions.Artificial Noise Test Group: Programmatically overlaid gradient-controllable noise (Gaussian white noise, low-frequency wind noise, intermittent pulse noise) at adjustable SNR tiers (5 dB, 0 dB, -5 dB) for fully quantified robustness comparison—a design absent in BirdNET's native workflow.Representative Stratified Subset: Dual-layer proportional stratified sampling to extract a balanced 2,000-clip subset from the full 12,400-record pool, with representativeness verification ensuring species proportion and noise distribution deviation within ±3%.This combination of three heterogeneous ML paradigms, dual-layer noise augmentation, and fully controllable SNR gradient noise experimentation cannot be found in BirdNET open-source code, Kaggle notebooks, or peer-reviewed bioacoustic papers, constituting the core novelty of this project.References[1] Kahl, S., Wood, C.
